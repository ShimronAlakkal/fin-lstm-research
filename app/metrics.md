## Solvency Metrics
A solvency ratio is a key metric used to measure an enterprise's ability to meet its long-term debt obligations and is used often by prospective business lenders. A solvency ratio indicates whether a company's cash flow is sufficient to meet its long-term liabilities and thus is a measure of its financial health.

#### Quick Ratio 
The quick ratio measures a company's ability to meet its short-term liabilities using its most liquid assets (assets that can be quickly converted to cash). It excludes inventory because it may not be as easily converted to cash in a short period. A higher quick ratio indicates a better ability to cover short-term obligations without relying on inventory sales.

#### Interest Coverage Ratio
The interest coverage ratio assesses a company's ability to meet its interest payments on outstanding debt. It indicates the company's ability to handle its interest obligations from its operating income. A higher ratio suggests a lower risk of defaulting on interest payments.

#### Debt to Networth Ratio
The debt to net worth ratio, also known as the leverage ratio, measures the proportion of a company's financing that comes from debt compared to shareholders' equity. It reflects the level of financial leverage used by the company. A high ratio may indicate higher financial risk, as it suggests a significant reliance on debt for funding.

#### Current Ratio
The current ratio assesses a company's short-term liquidity and its ability to meet its current (short-term) liabilities with its current assets. It provides an indication of the company's short-term financial health. A current ratio above 1 suggests the company has more current assets than current liabilities.




## Operations Efficiency Metrics
Operational efficiency metrics refer to measures used to assess how effectively a financial institution or organization is managing its operational resources and processes to achieve its objectives. These metrics are essential for evaluating the cost-effectiveness, productivity, and overall performance of an entity's operational activities. Operational efficiency is crucial in finance because it can directly impact profitability, risk management, and customer satisfaction. 

#### Sale To Receivable 
* Definition: This ratio measures how efficiently a company is collecting its accounts receivable (money owed by customers). It is calculated by dividing total sales by the average accounts receivable balance.
* Purpose: A higher ratio indicates that the company is collecting receivables quickly, which is a positive sign. A lower ratio may suggest that customers are taking longer to pay, which could lead to cash flow issues.

#### Avg. Days to Collect Account Receivables
* Definition: DSO measures the average number of days it takes for a company to collect payment after making a sale. It is calculated by dividing the average accounts receivable by average daily sales.
* Purpose: A lower DSO indicates faster collections, which can improve cash flow and reduce the risk of bad debt. A higher DSO suggests slower collections, which can strain liquidity.

#### Inventory Turnover
* Definition: Inventory turnover measures how efficiently a company manages its inventory. It is calculated by dividing the cost of goods sold (COGS) by the average inventory balance.
* Purpose: A higher inventory turnover ratio indicates that inventory is selling quickly, reducing storage costs and the risk of obsolescence. A lower ratio may imply overstocking or slow-moving inventory.

#### Avg. Days to Sell Inventory 
* Definition: This metric calculates the average number of days it takes for a company to turn its accounts receivable into cash. It is the inverse of the sales-to-receivables ratio.
* Purpose: A lower number of days to sell receivables implies quicker conversion of sales into cash, which is favorable for cash flow management.

* Definition: This measures the percentage change in a company's operating cash flow from one year to the next. Operating cash flow represents the cash generated or used by a company's core operations.

## Profitability Metrics 
Profitability metrics are financial ratios and measures used to assess a company's ability to generate profit from its operations and investments. These metrics provide insights into a company's financial health, performance, and its ability to generate returns for its shareholders.

#### Gross Profit Margin:
Formula: Gross Profit Margin = (Gross Profit / Total Revenue) × 100
Purpose: This metric measures the percentage of revenue that remains as gross profit after deducting the cost of goods sold (COGS). It indicates how efficiently a company produces and sells its products or services.

#### Operating Profit Margin (Operating Margin):
Formula: Operating Profit Margin = (Operating Profit / Total Revenue) × 100
Purpose: Operating margin measures the profitability of a company's core business operations by considering operating expenses. It reflects the efficiency of day-to-day operations.

#### Net Profit Margin (Net Margin):
Formula: Net Profit Margin = (Net Profit / Total Revenue) × 100
Purpose: Net profit margin represents the percentage of revenue that remains as net profit after accounting for all expenses, including taxes and interest. It provides a comprehensive view of a company's profitability.

#### Return on Assets (ROA):
Formula: ROA = (Net Profit / Total Assets) × 100
Purpose: ROA measures how effectively a company generates profit from its total assets. It indicates the efficiency of asset utilization in generating returns.

#### Return on Equity (ROE):
Formula: ROE = (Net Profit / Shareholders' Equity) × 100
Purpose: ROE assesses the return on investment for shareholders. It measures how efficiently a company generates profit from shareholders' equity.

#### Gross Profit 
Definition: Gross profit is the profit a company makes after subtracting the cost of goods sold (COGS) from its total revenue. Gross Profit Margin, expressed as a percentage, is calculated as (Gross Profit / Total Revenue) × 100.
Purpose: It measures the efficiency of a company's production and pricing. A higher gross profit margin indicates that a company can cover its operating expenses and make a profit more effectively.

#### Profit Before Taxes (PBT) Percent:
Definition: Profit before taxes represents a company's earnings before accounting for income tax expenses. It's a measure of a company's pre-tax profitability. Profit Before Taxes Percent is calculated as (Profit Before Taxes / Total Revenue) × 100.
Purpose: This metric provides insight into how much profit a company generates before considering its tax liabilities. It reflects the operational performance and efficiency in generating profits.

#### Percent Profit Before Tax to Total Assets:
Definition: This ratio evaluates a company's ability to generate profit in relation to its total assets. It's calculated as (Profit Before Taxes / Total Assets) × 100.
Purpose: It measures the efficiency of asset utilization in generating pre-tax profits. A higher percentage indicates that the company is effectively using its assets to generate earnings.

#### Percent Profit Before Tax to Net Worth (Shareholders' Equity):
Definition: This metric assesses the return on equity capital before considering taxes. It's calculated as (Profit Before Taxes / Shareholders' Equity) × 100.
Purpose: It indicates how efficiently a company generates pre-tax profit with the capital provided by shareholders. A higher percentage suggests effective use of equity capital
